A localized data roadmap for climate resilience planning in St. Thomas.

Post-hurricane and post-pandemic, DPNR had 600+ datasets scattered across the internet and no unified way to act on them. We designed a data strategy and built three working prototypes.

Key findings

What we learned

01
Localisation over standardisation
Off-the-shelf frameworks built for mainland US contexts don't translate to USVI. Starting from what already exists and is trusted was more durable than importing external standards.
02
The permitting bottleneck
Permitting data was the most frequently needed and the most fragmented. Digitising this one workflow had disproportionate impact across the entire planning process.
03
Community access is not optional
In a post-disaster context, community members need access to the same data as planners. The open data portal was as important as the internal dashboard for long-term resilience.
04
Gaps are information too
What was missing from the 600+ datasets, including real-time climate exposure and infrastructure status, was as actionable as what was there.

Research questions

01How can data analytics help St. Thomas with climate and environmental planning?
02What does a systemic, localised data framework look like for a small island context?
03How can local agencies leverage data to better understand and respond to climate change, and help vulnerable communities?

Background

Turning scattered data into actionable planning.

Following hurricanes Irma and Maria in 2017 and the COVID-19 pandemic, the US Virgin Islands needed to plan more effectively for climate resilience. DPNR had access to over 600 datasets covering demographics, climate stressors, infrastructure, and permitting, but they were spread across disconnected sources, maintained inconsistently, and largely inaccessible to the agencies and communities who needed them.

We accepted not a technical but an institutional challenge. How do you build data infrastructure that a small island government can actually sustain after a consulting team leaves?

Opportunities

01
High volume of projects. Construction in St. Thomas is at a historic volume, including coastal zone and shoreline projects requiring careful climate-risk review.
02
Public request for guidance. Residents have actively requested clearer regulation and more resilient planning practices.
03
Efficiency at DPNR. DPNR needs to process permits efficiently and in accord with best practices for minimizing climate-related losses.
04
Federal funding. Post-2017 recovery funds and the Infrastructure Investment Act offer resources for data-driven planning processes.
05
Centralised data-handling. A unified solution for data collection and sharing would benefit the entire government, not just DPNR.

Barriers

01Lack of standardization in data collection, processing, and sharing across arms of the USVI government.
02Insufficient place-based data for St. Thomas, limiting the quality of climate and planning analysis.
03Limited staffing capacity at DPNR to manage new digital tools without dedicated ongoing support.

Our approach

01
Opportunities and barriers
Mapped what DPNR had, what was missing, and what was realistic given the island's connectivity, staffing, and post-disaster context.
02
Stakeholder engagement
Worked with DPNR staff and community members to understand what data was actually used, what was trusted, and what needed to be publicly accessible.
03
Data roadmap
Produced a prioritised data roadmap: guidance on what to build, what to defer, and what to leave alone.
04
Prototype deliverables
Built three working prototypes designed for DPNR's actual capacity: a permitting dashboard, open data portal, and storymap.

Reflection

The hardest decisions were about what not to build.

Standard data infrastructure assumptions, including reliable connectivity, standardised formats, and institutional continuity, don't hold in a small island context recovering from back-to-back category 5 hurricanes.

The most important constraint wasn't budget or timeline. It was the question of what DPNR could actually maintain after we left. A powerful system that requires constant outside support is a new dependency, not resilience infrastructure.

Data infrastructure for a climate-stressed island is a governance problem, an equity problem, and a sustainability problem all at once.
