Core finding

Bay Area cities must actively integrate Bay Wheels into their public transit ecosystems through strategic pricing, infrastructure upgrades, and event-driven initiatives, transforming last-mile gaps into seamless, equitable connections for all residents.

Key messages

What the data shows across all three cities

01
Transit culture beats transit proximity
The strongest predictor of last-mile use is the share of public transit commuters in the surrounding census tract, not how close a station is to BART. Bay Wheels amplifies transit culture; it doesn't create it.
02
Three cities, three different stories
SF has diffuse ridership citywide. San Jose clusters tightly around Diridon with near-zero use elsewhere. East Bay shows moderate use even beyond transit proximity, driven by cycling culture and slow streets.
03
An equity gap runs through all three
Areas with higher White population consistently show higher last-mile usage across all three cities. Barriers to access in communities of colour require dedicated investigation and targeted intervention.
04
Classic bikes drive last-mile, e-bikes drive leisure
Classic bikes dominate short last-mile trips. E-bikes are chosen for longer recreational rides. Classic fleet availability at key transit-adjacent stations is the highest-leverage operational fix.
05
Weather is an underrated barrier
Wind significantly reduces last-mile trip probability. A weather-responsive rebalancing strategy at sheltered transit hubs on windy days could recover measurable ridership at low cost.
06
Members and casual riders behave differently
Members drive last-mile use in SF and East Bay. In San Jose, casual riders show higher last-mile probability, suggesting event-based expansion could be the entry point for new members there.

Predicted last-mile probability

Which stations are most likely to be last-mile hubs?

Logistic regression assigns each station a predicted probability of functioning as a last-mile hub. Yellow = high probability. Purple = low. Transit stations marked with × or triangles.

San Francisco · May 2025

FiDi and the eastern waterfront have the highest predicted probability. More variability across non-transit stations than the other cities, SF's diffuse transit network creates more opportunity outside the core.

San Jose · Sep 2024

Transit or nothing. Yellow stations cluster exclusively around Diridon. The rest of the network shows near-zero probability. Any expansion not anchored to transit is unlikely to generate last-mile use.

East Bay · Sep 2024

More nuance. High probability near BART, but moderate probability extends into Berkeley's slow-street corridors. Cycling culture itself is a predictor here, not just transit proximity.

San Francisco · May 2025

Near-transit stations serve commuters. Non-transit stations show more variety.

Bike stations near BART and Caltrain · dark dots within 500m · bubble size = trip count

Thursday is SF's peak day. Weekdays show commuter peaks at 8am and 6pm; weekends skew toward afternoon leisure. Rain, wind, and cold all reduce ridership noticeably.

Transit agencies have worked with Lyft to ensure station availability within ~0.25 miles of major stops, but coverage could be strengthened in underserved corridors outside the FiDi core.

SF is the only city where precipitation and wind speed were significant predictors in the regression model, suggesting weather-responsive rebalancing would have the highest ROI here.

Recommendation

Bundled MUNI/BART + Bay Wheels pricing. Event valets at Outside Lands and Pride to build casual-to-member conversion at scale.

San Jose · Sep 2024

Transit or nothing, the most binary city in the analysis.

Ridership near transit, parks, and universities · 500m transit buffer · bubble size = trip count
15 most popular stations, almost all within 1km of downtown Diridon

Sunday is San Jose's busiest day, the opposite of SF's Thursday peak. Weekday ridership peaks at ~6pm only, with minimal morning spike, suggesting a different rider profile.

Proximity to parks and universities did not predict last-mile use in San Jose. Recreational and student demand requires a separate product strategy from commuter demand.

Recommendations

Subsidised Clipper rates covering VTA + Bay Wheels. Trail integration along Guadalupe River and the Silicon Valley bike superhighway (now state-funded). Pop-up stations at Viva Calle, highest-usage day in Sep 2024 was Sept 8.

B. Tamien Caltrain, strong last-mile transit connectivity potential

East Bay · Sep 2024

Cycling culture extends last-mile use beyond transit corridors.

15 most popular East Bay stations, concentrated near BART; Dark blue circles are within 500m of a station

Monday is East Bay's busiest day. Weekday peaks at 6pm then 8am, a more balanced commuter profile than San Jose's single evening peak.

The most popular stations cluster near BART and UC Berkeley. East Bay's slow-street corridors and protected lanes create moderate last-mile probability even away from transit, a pattern unique to this city.

Casual riders drive last-mile use in East Bay more than in other cities, suggesting community cycling events are more effective tools here than commuter-focused pricing.

Recommendations

Clipper incentives for e-bike BART transfers. Pop-up stations at East Bay Bike Party. Expand stations along Berkeley's slow-street corridors to capture non-transit last-mile demand.

Three cities, three strategies

Recommendations

San Francisco
Moderate last-mile · High variability
Bundled transit pricing. Integrated Clipper + Bay Wheels pricing to reduce friction between the two systems.
Event discovery. Deploy valets at Outside Lands and Pride to convert casual users to committed members.
Non-transit framing. In non-transit SF neighbourhoods, lean into recreational use rather than forcing a last-mile framing the data doesn't support.
San Jose
High last-mile near transit · Near-zero elsewhere
Subsidised Clipper rates covering both VTA and Bay Wheels for low-income transit-dependent residents.
Trail integration along Guadalupe River Trail and the Silicon Valley bike superhighway, now state-funded.
Viva Calle pop-ups. The Sept 8 single-day spike confirms event-based expansion is the entry point for new users in San Jose.
East Bay
High last-mile near transit · Moderate elsewhere
E-bike transfer incentives. Clipper discounts for riders transferring from BART to e-bikes extend the effective service radius.
Slow-street corridor stations in Berkeley to capture non-transit last-mile demand that exists nowhere else in the Bay Area.
Festival-based expansion. East Bay Bike Party and local slow-street events are natural pop-up station venues.

Potential next steps

What this analysis opens up

SF: Event valet analysis
Study valet station performance at Outside Lands and Pride to guide future deployment, including whether valets create lasting membership conversion.
SJ and East Bay: Station placement
Analyse proximity to parks, restaurants, and trails to identify high foot/bike activity zones for new end-to-end station placements beyond the transit core.
All cities: Protected bike lanes
Evaluate the relationship between protected lane coverage and ridership to build the investment case for infrastructure improvements across all three cities.
